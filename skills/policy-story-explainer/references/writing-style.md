# Writing style

## Voice

Write in a warm, clear, human style.

Prefer:

* "Imagine this..."
* "In practice, this means..."
* "The important bit is..."
* "This does not mean..."
* "You would need approval when..."

Avoid:

* vague summaries
* motivational filler
* jokes that reduce seriousness
* over-casual language for sensitive topics
* changing mandatory language such as "must", "must not", "required", or "prohibited"

## Audience tuning

Ask who the explanation is for, then pitch the scenarios and depth accordingly. The policy rule
never changes — only the framing, examples, and level of detail do.

| Audience | Scenario style | Depth | What to emphasise |
|----------|----------------|-------|-------------------|
| **Employees / front line** | Everyday "this happened to you" moments | Light — one rule per theme | The action to take and the deadline |
| **Managers / approvers** | Decisions, sign-offs, exceptions | Medium — include approval routes and thresholds | When to escalate, what they own |
| **Auditors / compliance / legal** | Edge cases, conflicts, evidence trails | Heavy — keep clause refs and defined terms inline | Traceability, exceptions, what must be documented |

If the audience is mixed or unstated, default to the employee framing and add a short
"For managers / approvers" note under any theme that has an approval route or exception.

## Narrative continuity

For a long document, reuse one recurring person and organisation across themes instead of
inventing a new cast each time. Continuity makes a 30-page policy read like one coherent story
rather than a pile of disconnected vignettes.

* Introduce them once (e.g. "Maya, a new account manager at a mid-size firm") and let later themes
  reference the same characters as the situation escalates.
* Keep the cast small — one or two people and one org is enough.
* Never let the recurring scenario bend a rule to fit the story. The character serves the policy,
  not the other way round.

## Keep the narrative human

The story sections are where AI tells creep in. Before delivering, re-read the narrative prose —
the "realistic situation", "what the policy is saying", "what you should do", and "why it matters"
sections — and cut these patterns:

* Em-dash pile-ups and rule-of-three filler ("clear, concise, and compelling").
* "It's not just X, it's Y" and "not only... but also" constructions. State the point directly.
* Throat-clearing openers ("It's important to note that...", "In today's world...").
* Hollow intensifiers ("very", "really", "truly") and vague abstractions ("the implications are
  significant"). Name the specific thing.
* Metronomic rhythm. Vary sentence length; don't end every theme on a punchy one-liner.

Apply these edits to the narrative ONLY. Never touch "the actual policy point" blocks, quoted
definitions, or the executive summary: there, exact source wording and mandatory modals
(`must`/`shall`) outrank readable prose. "Fixing" passive voice or an extreme word inside a quoted
clause changes the policy.

## Anti-slop example (narrative only)

Edit the story sections; leave *The actual policy point* unchanged.

❌ **Before (AI tells in narrative):**

> *A realistic situation:* In today's fast-paced digital landscape, it's important to note that
> Maya — a dedicated, detail-oriented analyst — didn't just make a simple mistake; she sent
> sensitive, critical, and confidential data to the wrong recipient.

✅ **After:**

> *A realistic situation:* Maya emails a customer spreadsheet to the wrong supplier and notices the
> mistake that afternoon.
